## SECTION 2: DECISION-MAKING LANGUAGE

Mo's sales psychology is built on **disclosure, not pressure.** He frames information as "letting you in on something" rather than "closing you."

### PATTERN 1: Permission Framing ("If you want to...")

This removes pressure while still moving toward close. Positions Mo as collaborator, not salesman.

**Examples:**
- "If you want to keep your gold and not sell it to somebody, not a problem at all." (Video 7)
- "If you want to run some numbers, we can run some numbers." (Video 11)
- "If y'all want to, we can FaceTime you to confirm that it's real." (Video 11)

**Psychology:**
- Gives buyer complete agency
- Removes implied pressure
- Signals: "I'm okay with any outcome because I trust the logic"
- Actually INCREASES close rate because buyer feels in control

**How to Use:**
Every time you move toward ask or offer, frame it as "if you want to..."
- "If you want, we can pull the specs"
- "If interested, the next step is..."
- "If you want to see it in person, we're in [location]"

---

### PATTERN 2: Scarcity as Simple Binary

When urgency is genuine, frame it as a simple binary: act or miss. Never vague.

**Examples:**
- "Either you going to take it or you going to lose it." (Video 8)
- "If the video is up, it's still available." (Clean, simple availability signal)
- "This deal will only last while supplies last. I got four of these, seven of these, eight of these." — Specific numbers signal honesty

**Psychology:**
- Specific inventory counts = credibility
  - Saying "tons" sounds like lying
  - Saying "four, seven, eight" sounds like you actually counted
- Binary choice removes hedging and confusion
- Creates urgency without desperation

**How to Use:**
- Always give actual count ("I got [X]") instead of "limited"
- Frame as choice, not pressure ("either/or" not "you should")
- Update counts as inventory moves (real-time honesty)
- Clean, simple: "If post is up, we got some. If post is down, they're gone."

---

### PATTERN 3: The Trust Bridge ("You Get All Your Materials Back")

Used when customers hesitate about handing over gold or materials.

**Examples:**
- "You get all the materials back. You just pay a small fee." (Videos 1, 7)
- "We take the materials, remake it, and you get something beautiful."

**Psychology:**
- Reframes transaction from "spending money" to "upgrading what you own"
- Removes fear of loss
- Makes customer feel they're just paying for labor, not buying new stuff

**How to Use:**
- Early in custom service conversations
- When customer seems hesitant about sending in materials
- As reassurance: "You're not losing anything; you're transforming it"

---

### PATTERN 4: Definitive vs. Flexible Language Cues

Give customers clear signals about what's negotiable vs. non-negotiable.

**Definitive (Non-Negotiable Floor):**
- "The best I could do on that is 1425."
- "Three G's is off. Nobody's going to offer that."
- No hedging, no "well maybe" — this is the floor

**Flexible (Cooperative Zone):**
- "We go up and down 50, 100, 200, 300..."
- "We can run some numbers."
- Signals: I'm willing to find the mutual zone

**Definitive-But-Fair (Setting Accountability):**
- "I'm not trying to work against you. You got to be fair with me."
- Mutual accountability frame

**Psychology:**
- Definitive statements prevent endless renegotiation
- Flexible signals prevent buyer feeling trapped
- Combination signals: "I'm firm but not unfair"

**How to Use:**
- For price floor: make one final offer, state it definitive once
- For timeline/payment: signal flexibility
- For quality standards: never budge (firm, protective)
- For customization options: show range you can work with

---

### PATTERN 5: Core Philosophy as Catchphrase

**"Get your money up, not your funny up."**

Appears 6+ times across 15 videos with slight variations: "not your funny," "not your funny son," "not your funny up."

**What This Does:**
1. Motivates audience to save/invest properly
2. Positions prices as aspirational but achievable
3. Warns against cheap/fake/mediocre choices
4. Creates collective vocabulary audience can use and repeat
5. Encodes complete worldview: *Save right, spend on real value, don't waste.*

**Why It Works:**
- Rhyme scheme is memorable and repeatable
- Audience begins using phrase themselves (proof of adoption)
- Applies to his buying from vendors AND his selling to customers
- Simultaneously motivates AND justifies price point

**How to Use:**
- After price reveals (especially deals)
- When motivating someone to invest in quality
- When pushing back against "that's too expensive" objections
- As closing phrase in promotional moments
- Creates community: people who "get" this phrase are your people

## SECTION 3: HUMOR & WIT TAXONOMY

Mo's humor is **conversational, deadpan, and confessional.** Punchlines land on END of sentences, never beginnings. He doesn't set up jokes; jokes emerge from actual situations.

### TYPE 1: The Confident Undersell

Use hyperbolic understatement when value is obvious. The contrast between graphic energy and calm narration IS the joke.

**Example:**
- "STEAL!!!!" (screaming red text) + calm voice saying "This is a 10 karat..." = contrast = humor
- Audience laughs because the graphics scream but the person is chill — acknowledgment that the hype-to-reality gap is absurd

**How to Use:**
- When value is genuinely obvious, use ironic understatement
- Let graphics do hype; keep voice calm
- Trust audience gets the joke (no explanation needed)

---

### TYPE 2: The Coin Flip / Roulette Gambit

When price gap becomes an impasse, offer a game to defuse tension.

**Examples:**
- "Spin the chamber. Spin the roulette. Whatever you want."
- "Coin flip, we do coin flip, too. See?"

**Psychology:**
- Defuses tension by introducing novelty/humor
- Makes buyer laugh instead of frustrated

**How to Use:**
- Only when negotiation is genuinely stuck and you need to walk away
- Use humor to keep relationship intact despite hard "no"
- Don't overuse; it loses power if deployed every time
- Works because audience recognizes it's absurdist theater, not actual recklessness

**Psychology:**

- Converts potential sales friction into likability

**How to Use:**

- Maintains relationship despite doubt
